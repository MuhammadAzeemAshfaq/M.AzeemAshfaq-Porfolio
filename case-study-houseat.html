The Problem

Finding a property and a compatible roommate are two separate problems that existing platforms solve badly

Rental platforms focus on listings but do nothing to help tenants figure out who they will actually be living with. Roommate finder tools are disconnected from property listings and rely entirely on users self-reporting their habits, which leads to mismatches and conflicts down the line.

On the other side, landlords managing multiple properties were juggling separate tools for listings, tenant communication, rent tracking, and maintenance requests. Agents had no dedicated workspace to manage their role as intermediaries between landlords and tenants.

The client wanted a single platform that solved all of this across four distinct user types, including an admin layer to oversee and manage everything.

The Four Roles

One platform, four distinct experiences

Tenant

Browse property listings, complete a habit survey, get matched with compatible roommates through AI scoring, and apply for properties.

Landlord

List and manage properties, review tenant applications, handle rent collection, and track maintenance requests from a single dashboard.

Agent

Act as intermediary between landlords and tenants, manage multiple listings on behalf of landlords, and facilitate the full rental process.

Admin

Oversee the entire platform from a central dashboard. Manage all users across every role, moderate listings, resolve disputes, and configure platform-wide settings.

My Approach

Building the AI roommate matching system

The most technically interesting part of this project was the roommate matching engine. When a tenant signs up they complete a survey covering daily habits, sleep schedule, social preferences, cleanliness standards, and lifestyle. These answers are converted into a compatibility vector and scored against other tenants looking at the same property.

  • The survey was designed with a product psychologist to capture habits that actually predict roommate compatibility rather than surface-level preferences.
  • Matching scores were calculated using weighted cosine similarity across habit categories, with different weights applied based on research into which differences cause the most conflict.
  • Tenants see a compatibility percentage and a short explanation of where they match well and where there are differences, so they can make an informed decision rather than relying on a number alone.

Role-based access control was one of the most complex parts to get right. Tenants, landlords, agents, and the admin each have a fundamentally different view of the same underlying data. Getting the permission model clean and secure early on prevented a lot of headaches later. The admin role in particular required careful scoping so it had full visibility without accidentally exposing sensitive tenant or landlord data outside of its intended context.

What I Learned

Key takeaways

Building for multiple user roles on a single platform is a product design challenge as much as a technical one. The navigation, language, and information hierarchy need to feel native to each role even though they all run on the same underlying system. With four distinct roles including an admin with platform-wide access, spending time on role-specific user flows before writing code was what made this work.

The AI matching felt much more trustworthy to users when we showed them the reasoning behind a score rather than just showing the number. Explainability turned out to matter a lot for adoption.
